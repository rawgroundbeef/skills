## How to use the catalog

Treat these as prompts for judgment, not a compliance checklist. Select the few patterns that address the page's largest clarity, trust, or action bottleneck. A page can violate a pattern and still be correct for its audience, economics, brand, or sales motion.

When patterns conflict, protect this order:

1. Truth and user trust
2. Accessibility and comprehension
3. Product and business-model fit
4. Conversion focus
5. Memorability and novelty

## Positioning and recall

### Own one memorable job

Make the product easy to describe in one short sentence and easy to associate with one important outcome. Multiple capabilities may support that job; do not market them as unrelated products.

### Sell the human outcome

Lead with gained time, money, health, confidence, status, control, or relief when that outcome is real. Features explain how the outcome happens; they are not the destination.

### Use language the audience already uses

Mine actual interviews, support messages, reviews, searches, and sales calls for the problem's vocabulary. Combine customer language with details only this founder or product could truthfully say.

### Prefer specifics over fog

Replace words such as “fast,” “powerful,” or “many” with a concrete behavior, limit, comparison, or measured result. A number is not automatically stronger: include its cohort, timeframe, and source when those affect meaning.

### Make the headline plain and sticky

Use familiar words and a sentence structure a non-expert can repeat. Generate several materially different candidates. Test immediate comprehension first and next-day recall second; cleverness that hides the product fails.

### Attach to existing demand

Connect the offer to a technology, behavior, or problem people already discuss when the connection is durable and genuine. Do not distort the roadmap to borrow a temporary trend.

## Attention and hierarchy

### Make the hero stand on its own

Assume some visitors will decide from the first section. Show the product category, audience, outcome, primary action, and a reason to believe. This does not mean cramming the full sales page above the fold.

### Give each section one dominant idea

Use visual hierarchy and progressive disclosure so each section advances one part of the decision. Interpret “one screen” responsively; viewport height varies by device, zoom, language, and accessibility settings.

### Restrain the palette

Use neutral surfaces, readable text, and a deliberate accent to make the primary action obvious. “Three colors” is an attention heuristic, not a literal cap: preserve brand needs, accessible contrast, links, focus indicators, errors, and status semantics.

### Finish intentionally

Use the footer area for more than administrative links. Restate the strongest idea, offer a useful artifact or shareable moment, and provide the same clear next step without hiding necessary legal and support navigation.

## Demonstration and proof

### Show the product before explaining every feature

Prefer a real screenshot, short workflow, interactive sample, generated result, or before-and-after demonstration. Choose the smallest demo that proves the core promise.

### Let visitors experience value safely

Expose a meaningful sample or interaction on the page when feasible. This can coexist with paid access: demonstrate the mechanism without giving away a costly complete outcome.

### Describe the problem with empathy

Show that the team understands the visitor's context, stakes, and failed alternatives before pressing the sale. Avoid melodrama and invented pain.

### Use authentic social proof

Launch with credible proof when trust is a meaningful barrier, but never fabricate it. A small number of specific beta-user observations is stronger than anonymous praise. Make the relationship to the quoted person clear.

### Compare on buyer-relevant criteria

Use a concise comparison when visitors are choosing among known alternatives. Include the criteria that drive their decision, acknowledge meaningful tradeoffs, date the research, and link evidence. Do not design a rigged table of trivial checkmarks.

## Action and pricing

### Choose one primary next step

Make one conversion path visually dominant. Secondary links may support evaluation or returning users, but they should not compete at equal weight.

### Name what the CTA does

Prefer an action such as “Analyze my site,” “See the live demo,” or “Buy the desktop app” over a vague “Get started.” Match the label to the immediate next screen; do not disguise signup or payment.

### Make the price easy to find

Expose pricing in navigation or early decision context when the product has public self-serve pricing. For negotiated enterprise sales, explain the pricing basis and buyer path rather than inventing a number.

### Minimize packaging decisions

Use the fewest options that map to real customer segments. Three tiers can create a useful good/better/best anchor, but one, two, or usage-based choices may be clearer. Remove differences customers do not understand or value.

## Trust and distinctiveness

### Put a human behind the product

Use a founder demo, explanation, or story when founder credibility is relevant. A visible person is a trust mechanism only when their presence adds evidence, context, or accountability.

### Create a legitimate surprise

Give visitors something they have not seen in every competitor: a revealing demo, opinionated point of view, unusually transparent proof, distinctive interaction, or memorable language. Novelty must reinforce the product's job.

### Use a memorable, comprehensible name

Prefer familiar sounds and words that are easy to hear, spell, search, and repeat. A coined name can work when the brand has resources to teach it; do not treat wordplay as free differentiation.

## Distribution and sharing

### Treat the social preview as an advertisement

Design the OG image for a small, crowded feed: one legible promise, a strong product or outcome visual, recognizable branding, and minimal text. Inspect the rendered crop on target platforms when possible.

### Make sharing rewarding

Offer an insight, result, comparison, visual, or line that helps the visitor express something by sharing it. Do not confuse manipulative referral gates with organic share value.

## Context gates

Apply these before turning an aggressive pattern into a recommendation.

### Free plan versus paid entry

Favor paid entry when free usage has material delivery or support cost, attracts the wrong segment, or delays willingness-to-pay validation. Favor freemium or a free utility when marginal cost is low and network effects, collaboration, user-generated content, or product-led distribution create compounding value. Consider a limited trial or on-page demo separately from a permanent free tier.

### Hard paywall versus demonstration

Charging before a costly or complete outcome can validate demand. Hiding comprehension behind registration cannot. Let visitors see the mechanism and understand the result before asking for data or payment unless privacy, abuse, or delivery cost makes that unsafe.

### Subscription versus one-time payment

Use recurring pricing when the product delivers recurring value and incurs ongoing operations, data, content, support, or infrastructure costs. Use one-time pricing when the delivered value is substantially finite and future support remains sustainable. Do not choose either model merely because it is easier to market.

### Premium versus parity pricing

Charge a premium when differentiated value, buyer ROI, proof, and support justify it. Price competitively when adoption, standardization, switching cost, or an undifferentiated market makes premium positioning implausible. “More expensive” is not itself a benefit.

### One CTA versus complete navigation

Maintain one dominant conversion action without deleting paths required for existing customers, detailed evaluation, accessibility, support, privacy, or legal compliance.

## Evidence and safety rules

- Trace numerical claims to product data or an explicit source. Record cohort, timeframe, and methodology when material.
- Keep illustrative examples visibly hypothetical; never present them as customer outcomes.
- Use testimonials and logos only with a real source and appropriate permission. Do not invent, composite, or silently strengthen quotes.
- Date competitor comparisons and cite first-party evidence where possible. Re-check them before publication.
- Avoid unsupported superlatives, scarcity, countdowns, guarantees, and “risk-free” language.
- Preserve accessible contrast, focus states, semantic structure, keyboard use, reduced motion, and readable text while simplifying visual hierarchy.
- Treat pricing, packaging, paywall, refund, and billing changes as cross-functional product decisions with legal and operational implications.
- Define the intended signal before an experiment: primary metric, guardrail, audience, and decision rule. Do not report causality from an uncontrolled before-and-after observation.
